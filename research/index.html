Research

An overview of research in the Heberle Lab

Overview

The plasma membrane (PM) is a two-lipid-molecule-thick sheet that separates the cell from its environment. Far from being a passive barrier, the PM is a highly dynamic and heterogeneous structure whose lateral and transverse organization is intimately linked to cell signaling, membrane trafficking, and disease.

Our group applies biophysical, biochemical, and computational approaches to understand how the chemical diversity of membrane lipids gives rise to functional membrane structure. A central question we address is how lipids with distinct physical properties organize into nanoscale domains — often called lipid rafts — and how these structures participate in cell biology.

Model Systems

We use model membrane systems that allow us to systematically vary composition and isolate the physical determinants of membrane organization:

  • Unilamellar vesicles (ULVs) — the simplest model, allowing precise control over lipid composition and the use of scattering techniques
  • Giant unilamellar vesicles (GUVs) — large enough to image directly by fluorescence or electron microscopy
  • Asymmetric vesicles — engineered to mimic the lipid asymmetry of the natural plasma membrane (inner vs. outer leaflet differ in composition)
  • Plasma membrane vesicles (PMVs) — derived directly from living cells, capturing near-native complexity

Techniques

Small-Angle Neutron and X-ray Scattering (SANS / SAXS)

Scattering methods provide quantitative structural information at the nanometer scale without the need for labels or stains. We use contrast variation SANS — a technique that exploits the difference in neutron scattering between hydrogen and deuterium — to selectively highlight different membrane components and detect nanoscale domains that are invisible to optical microscopy.

Much of our SANS work is performed at the Spallation Neutron Source (SNS) and High Flux Isotope Reactor (HFIR) at Oak Ridge National Laboratory.

Cryogenic Electron Microscopy (Cryo-EM)

Cryo-EM allows us to image membrane structure at near-atomic resolution. We recently published the first direct images of lipid nanodomains using cryo-EM, providing structural evidence for nanoscale lateral organization in model membranes.

Fluorescence Spectroscopy and Microscopy

Fluorescence techniques — including FRET, fluorescence lifetime imaging (FLIM), and confocal microscopy — provide complementary information about domain formation, membrane dynamics, and lipid–protein interactions.

Differential Scanning Calorimetry (DSC)

DSC measures the thermodynamics of membrane phase transitions, providing information about how membrane composition affects the stability and coexistence of different lipid phases.
